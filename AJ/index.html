Jamie's Journey from Hungary to Latvia

Before I left Hungary, I took a day trip to nearby Palić in Serbia. The train between Szeged and Subotica stops here but had been out of service until recently.

Lake Palić was a popular summer resort in the old days of Yugoslavia.

This is one of the banquet halls along the lake.

Back in Szeged, I washed my bike and put it up for sale as it would have been too much to bring with me on the train and ship to Latvia.

This is the University of Szeged, just one block from my apartment. The banners on the building honor Dr. Katalin Karikó who was awarded a Nobel Prize last year for her work in mRNA research. She received both her undergraduate degree as well as her PhD here. However, she is not the first Nobel laureate from the university as Dr. Albert Szent-Györgyi received the award in 1937 after isolating ascorbic acid (vitamin C) for the first time here.

This is the water tower in my neighborhood, built in 1904. There are no mountains or even hills in this part of Europe (the Pannonian Basin, sometimes called the Great Hungarian Plain) so water towers are vital.

My last geocache in Hungary was found under this cross along the River Mureş

One last look at my apartment before heading out to the train station.

Everything I have fits into my backpack and this suitcase.

I took the train from Szeged to Budapest and then caught another train to Austria from this station.

My first stop was in Linz, Austria. I hadn't been to Austria in 15 years, almost to the day.

I guess this is the smallest geocache I've ever found!

One of the wheels on my suitcase decided not to continue on the voyage.

Austria and Germany have changed quite a lot, demographically speaking, and you'll hear no complaints from me about the food on offer!

These trains are not only accessible but convenient when traveling with a suitcase. This is Hamburg.

From the port called Travemünde in northern Germany, I got aboard the Stena Livia, which serves not only as a passenger ferry, but a cargo ship as well.

I wasn't listening to anything in my headphones, my ears were just cold.

This was my cabin.

I brought along my radio and could listen to the crew on the bridge as well as weather reports from the coast guard.

This picture was taken in the middle of the Baltic Sea, somewhere south of Gotland in Sweden.

Once I arrived in the city of Liepāja, Latvia, I was able to find an apartment right away, which was required to apply for my visa. Everything in the apartment is brand new.

What I love about this apartment is that it overlooks a lake, so it's perfect for birdwatching and sunrises.

I'm also very close to the beach.

There is no Aldi here, but I have successfully converted to Lidlism.

On many an afternoon, there are kite surfers out on the lake, which I think is crazy because it's not exactly warm out there.

Liepāja is a quiet place.

Another view of the sand dunes and beach.

Geocaching led me to discover this pet cemetery nearby.

Liepāja will turn 400 next year, and in 2027 will be European Capital of Culture, along with Évora in Portugal.

A video I made about the tunnels beneath Seoul will be featured at an urban exploration film festival in Korea this month. It was highlighted during an online presentation at the Royal Asiatic Society last month.

Liepāja's tram system, dating back to the 1800s, offers rides for just 85 cents.

This church is in the district of Liepāja called Karosta, which in the Soviet days was a closed military base.

The Latvian Navy, stationed here, plays a crucial role in removing undetonated sea mines from World War II scattered across the Baltic seabed.
